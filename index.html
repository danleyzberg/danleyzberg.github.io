Before coming to Princeton and while I was still working on my Ph.D., I taught high school computer science classes part-time for six years at Hamden Hall Country Day School, a private preparatory K-12 school in Hamden, Connecticut. These classes were often the only exposure my students had to CS, besides summer camps and independent pursuits. I taught AP Computer Science A -- a Java-based course that covers about 45% of the material in COS 126, and I taught a course similar to COS 109 called Computer Science Principles, which is soon to be offered as an AP test by the College Board. Ph.D. student at Yale University, advised by Brian Scassellati. I build models of individual learning differences in order to build robot tutors that personalize their instruction to suit the particular needs of individual students. This is a difficult problem to solve, even if a content expert defines the skills that the robot is assessing and teaching in advance, because the robot must decode the many-to-many relationship between the observations it makes of the student and the internal skills those observations represent. For instance, a calculus tutor who observes a student incorrectly solve an integral equation faces the non-trivial task of assessing which skills the student may need to review. It is a non-trivial task because more than one skill can be necessary for a single step in a calculus problem and each step a student takes may reflect proficiency in one of many skills.
