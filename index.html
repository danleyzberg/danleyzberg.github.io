Teaching

This will be my first semester at Princeton. I will be precepting for COS 126. I'll also be working on some software tools for computer science education that will, hopefully, one day be used by thousands of students all over the world. If you're interested in changing the way high schoolers learn to program, contact me!

Before I came to Princeton, while I was still working on my Ph.D., I taught high school computer science classes part-time for six years at Hamden Hall Country Day School. I taught "AP Computer Science A" -- which covers about 40% of the material in COS 126, and I taught a course similar to COS 109 called "Computer Science Principles," which was inspired by the new AP course of the same name currently in development. I also used to teach a summer "Introduction to Robotics" for grades 4-8.

Research

You may know me from from a past SIGCSE conference. Right now I'm very interested in creating tools for computer science education. I have a few projects in the works at the moment on this topic. My goal is to get thousands of students to use them, all over the world. I think the tools we use to introduce students to programming affect their perception of computer science for years to come. Making good tools would increase participating in computer science by a wider range of students.

I'm also very interested in the public and layman perception of computer science as a discipline. We have come a long way since the 70's and 80's but there is still a lot to do.

In a past life, I was a robotics researcher. I got my Ph.D. (pending) from Yale University, advised by Brian Scassellati. I built robot tutors that adapted to individual students' needs. models of individual learning differences in order to build robot tutors that personalize their instruction to suit the particular needs of individual students. This is a difficult problem to solve, even if a content expert defines the skills that the robot is assessing and teaching in advance, because the robot must decode the many-to-many relationship between the observations it makes of the student and the internal skills those observations represent. For instance, a calculus tutor who observes a student incorrectly solve an integral equation faces the non-trivial task of assessing which skills the student may need to review. It is a non-trivial task because more than one skill can be necessary for a single step in a calculus problem and each step a student takes may reflect proficiency in one of many skills.
