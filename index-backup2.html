Welcome to my homepage! My name is Dan Leyzberg and I am a member of the faculty in the Computer Science Department at the fabulous Princeton University. I am a Lecturer, which means I get paid to do what I love, teach computer science to some of the brightest young people on the planet!

On this page, you can learn almost everything there is to know about me, including where I went to school, what classes I'm teaching and what I've taught in the past, who I've mentored, and what projects I'm currently spending my time on.

Should you find yourself wanting to get in touch, please feel free to drop me a line at dan.leyzberg@princeton.com or find your way to my office in room 208 in the Computer Science Building at 35 Olden Street in Princeton, NJ.

Teaching

Hello Princetonians! This is my first semester here at Princeton and I am very excited to be precepting for COS 126. In addition to precepting, I'm also helping Sandra Batista, a fellow COS Lecturer, advise junior and senior COS majors in their independent work as part of Facebook's Open Academy program, in which students are matched with open-source software development teams to get real-world software dev. experience by contributing to their codebases. In addition to those two things, I'm also very interested in developing software to help students and teachers better communicate about code. If you're interested in any of those three things, come chat!

Before coming to Princeton, back while I was working on my Ph.D., I taught high school computer science. For six years, I taught two of my own CS courses, at the lovely Hamden Hall Country Day School, a fancy private school in Connecticut. I taught "AP Computer Science A" (my old syllabus) -- which covers about a third of the material in Princeton's COS 126, as well as an introductory course called "Computer Science Principles," based on the new AP course in development by the College Board. For two summers, I also taught an "Introduction to Robotics" class for grades 4-8 at Hamden Hall's Summer Academy.

Mentoring

I take mentoring rather seriously, which is to say I'm very hands-on and involved. Below are the best and brightest students I've had the pleasure of working with. If you're a Princeton COS major, I would be happy to advise your IW.

Sam Spaulding joined the Yale Social Robotics Lab as a freshman in 2010 and collaborated with me on a number of projects and papers. He presented one of our papers at the prestigious Human Robot Interaction conference in 2014. As an aside, he played Jeopardy and "Who Wants to be a Millionaire?" on TV and did very well for himself. Sam is currently working on his Ph.D. at MIT.

Jenny Liu was a junior at Amity High School when she first came to the Yale Social Robotics Lab. She contributed to one of my earliest projects in grad school. She was a coauthor on the resulting publication, and she won 9th place nationally at the 2011 Intel Science Fair presenting her work! Jenny is now a Computer Science concentrator at Harvard.

Peter DeLong was a stand-out student in my AP Computer Science class in 2011. He and I worked on a summer project that became a very useful tool for high school students and teachers to find good practice problems for Math League competitions called GNHML. It is still in wide use by students and teachers from all over the district today! Peter matriculated at Princeton in 2014.

Educational Software

I am particularly interested in building tools for teachers and students to better communicate about code. I am going to be maintaining internal Princeton tools used in COS 126 like the Java Visualizer built by Dave Pritchart. Then, I'd like to build more generalizable versions of this software and other tools so other teachers and classes can use them. I'm especially interested in targeting high school AP Computer Science classes, because I think there still aren't enough good web tools for teachers in that space.

Research

I did robotics research in grad school. Or, more specifically, Human-Robot Interaction (HRI) research. I got my Ph.D. (pending) from Yale University, advised by Dr. Brian Scassellati. I built robot tutors that adapted to individual students' needs by building models of individual learning differences upon which robot tutors could personalize their instruction. I'm still happy to advise students in this area, or more broadly in Human-Computer Interaction (HCI). If you're interested in this work, please let me know.
